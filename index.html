Buses in Ireland

Some facts about bus services in Ireland:

  • Dublin bus, Bus Eireann and the Airport Bus (The Green Bus) are the most popular bus services that operate in Ireland.
  • There is a fee for all passengers except for those 6 or over.
  • Guide dogs are allowed to travel for free but no other animals are allowed.
  • It is illegal to smoke onboard
  • Passengers are allowed to carry 1 item of hand luggage free of charge.
  • To carry a bicycle on the bus for a single journey, it costs e11.
  • Ireland's first bus services began in 1971.
  • Bus Eireann has the most services in the Republic of Ireland outside of Dublin.
  • Irish citizens depend on buses for school trips, school tours and to get around their local area around Ireland.

Bus services for young people in Ireland:

  • Bus Eireann and Dublin Bus facilitate college students with several routes to colleges every day.
  • City and town routes serve colleges in general areas.
  • Secondary School students can get schoolchild and scholar photo cards, which allows them to get certain Dublin Bus Student tickets.
  • Buses facilitate the needs and wants of young people. Most bus services in Ireland have free wifi-zones, DVD and radio players, tinted windows, arm rests, televisions, and curtains on the windows.
  • The green bus has even more advanced facilities including climate control, leather seats and increased seat spacing for superior comfort.

Christmas in Ireland

Christmas traditions in Ireland

A light in the window

One old custon that many continue to observe is the placing of a candle in the window on Christmas Eve, a symbol to welcome strangers and to rememberthose who are far away from home. Light shining through the windows on Christmas Eve gives a warm and welcoming feeling when walking through a town on Christmas Eve.

Midnight Mass on Christmas Eve

If a church packed to the rafters is something youre looking for, look no further than any church in Ireland at midnight mass on Christmas Eve. This is a huge social gathering where family, friends and neighbours who you have not seen all year come together to celebrate Christmas.

With Christmas carols being sung annd often, live music being played, midnight mass in Ireland is a great place to catch up with old friends and get in touch with the local community.

Horce racing on St. Stephen's Day

St. Stephen is the patron saint of horses but I am almost positive that this is not the reason that horse racing on St. Stephen's Day has become a traditon in Ireland. The races in Leopardstown, South Dublin, attract almost 20,000 people every year, but I dont' think it has anything ot do with the old Germanic tradition of racing horses on St. Stephen's Day to honour the saint.

In Ireland heading off to the races is a chance to get out of the house, stretch your legs, look at the horses and have a drink with friends.

Christmas Day Swim, Forty Foot, South Dublin

Christmas day swims take place all over Ireland on Christmas morning, but most famously at the forty foot rock, just south of Dublin. On Christmas Day hundreds of peoplecan be seen jumping into the Irish Sea wearing only bathing suits.

The water in the Irish Seaon Christmas Day is usually around 10C (50F). Unfortunately, the temprature outside the water is usually about half of this, making the experience bracing to say the least. This is certainly not for the faint hearted but is a proven hangover cure, and participants often receive sponsorship for charities.

Decorations/ Holly Wreathes

Christmas decoration in Ireland traditionally was just a wreath of holly on the front of the door of the house. However, National Lampoon's, "Christmas Vacation" seems to be have been a blueprint for many Irish households as they are now lit up like Rockefeller Center.

Also traditionally, decorations would go up on December 8 the Feast of the Immaculate Conception and also come down on Little Christmas, January 6th.

A Christmas Whitewashing

Around Christmas, you'll still find the odd farm building in the Irish coutryside that looks like it's just been whitewashed. Long ago, farm families cleaned and then whitewashed every farm building in December. They were all covered in white paint or limewash, to symbolically purify them for the coming of the saviour. The tradition traces back thousands of years, not just through Celtic culture but through other central European cultures.

Bonfires

Originally... Bonfires were used to ward off bad luck or evil spirits. The whole community gathered on Halloween night around the bonfire. The next day everyone cleared out their fires at home and sprinkled ashes from their own fire and the bonfire in their fields as a form of protection and good crops for the next year.

Today... Bonfires have no important meaning for most people and are just used for fun. However, they are dangerous if they out of control. Lots of waste material or old furniture is burnt in bonfires now

Fireworks: Fireworks are commonly used in Ireland to celebrate Halloween. People gather together near where they live to watch fireworks usually but some travel to see firework displays. Black cats are a type of firework and are popular with young people in Ireland. They make a loud bang and light up but they don't shoot up in the air and they are not colourful.

Trick or treating in Ireland

Years ago... Poor people used to go to richer people's houses on Halloween and were given food if the poor person would agree to say prayers for the homeowner's dead relatives or friends.

Today... Children dress up in costumes and go from house to house telling jokes and singing songs in return for sweets, nuts, fruit and small change.

Working Hours in Ireland

Ireland's working hours

The Organisation of Working Time Act 1997 states that the maximum average working week for many employees cannot exceed 48 hours. This does not mean that a working week can never exceed 48 hours, it is the average that is important. The average Irish working week is 39 hours. The standard irish office day is from 9am until 5.30pm with an hour for lunch, taken between 12 and 2pm. Many officesincluding government departments, are closed between 12.30 and 2pm.

The average may be calculated in one of the following ways:

  • Over four months for most employees.
  • Over six months for employees working in the security industry, hospitals, prisons, gas/electricity, airports/docks, agriculture and employees in business which have peak periods at certain times of the year such as tourism.
  • Over 12 months where there has been an agreement between the employer and the employees to this effect. The agreement between the employer and the employee must be approved by the Labour Court.

The calculation of 48 hours does not include annual leave, sick leave and maternity/adoptive/parental leave.

The provisions of the Organisation of Working Time Act 1997 on working time and rest periods do not apply to all employees. They do not apply to the Gardaí, Defence Forces, employees who control their own working hours or family employees on farms or in private homes.

If you do Sunday work your entitlement to extra pay may be agreed between you and the employer. Under the Organisation of Working Time Act, if there is no agreement about your pay, your employer must give you one or more of the following for Sunday working:

  • A reasonable allowance
  • A reasonable pay increase
  • Reasonable paid time off work

Breaks

The general rule on breaks is that you are entitles to a break of 15 minutes aftert a four and a half hour work period. If you work more than 6 hours you are entitled to a break of 30 minutes, which can include the first 15 minute break. There is no entitlement to be paid during these breaks and they are not considered part of working time. Shop employees who work more than 6 hours and whose hours of work include 11.30am-2.30pm are entitled to a one hour consecutive break which must occur during those hours.

The Irish Constitution in Ireland

The Irish Constitution

The Constitution of Ireland (Bunreacht na hEireann) is the fundamental law of Ireland. It came into force on 29 December 1937 following national plebiscite held on 1 July 1937.

The President of Ireland

The current President of Ireland (Uachtaran na hEireann) is Michael D. Higggins. He took office on the 11 November 2011. Aras an Uachtarain in Dublin, Ireland is the offical residence of the President of Ireland.

The President plays a mainly ceremonial role in the running of the State. The Taoiseach of Ireland is the Prime Minister. The current Taoiseach is Enda Kenny. The Taoisech answers the questions in the Dail where broad policy is concerned, or where issues specific to his department are concerned. The Taoiseach may also sponsor legislation which represents important new developments.

The Irish Economy

The Irish Economy

The economy of Ireland is a modern knowledge economy. The Irish economy is a small and highly open. The value of internationally traded goods and services in 2012 was equivalent to 191% of GDP, which amounted to Euro 164 billion for the year. Services are the largest component of Irish output: in 2012 they accounted for 67% of gross value added at the factor cost, while industry and agriculture represented 31% and 2% of gross value added.

Pharmaceutical products, computer and electronic products, and food accounted for 34.5%, 17.2% and 16.5% of total gross industrial output in 2009. The Financial Crisis of 2008 affected the Irish economy severely, compounding domestic economic problems related to the collapse of the Irish property bubble

Until 2007 the levels of Irish public debt were low and declining. Ireland's national debt fell from 34% of GDP in 2000 to 25% of GDP in 2007. As a consequence of the very deep recession which began in 2008, combined with significant levels of public money injected into the Irish banking system in 2009, 2010 and 2011, the level of public debt soared. National debt, increased from 20% of GDP in 2007 to 84% of GDP in 2012, and the general government debt increased from 25% of GDP in 2007 to 117% of GDP in 2012

As a small, trade-dependent economy, Ireland's ability to recover from the crisis depends highly on the future of it's trading partners and their ability to recover from the Great Recession and the European debt crisis. It was the first country in the EU to officially enter a recession related to the Financial crisis 2008. In November 2010 the Irish Government published the National Recovery plan, which aimed to restore order to the public finances and to bring it's deficit in line with the EU target of 3% of economic output by 2015

Irish Schools

Irish Schooling System

All children must receive compulsory education between the ages of five and sixteen years, and all children up to the age of eighteen must complete the three years of post-primary. The Constitution of Ireland allows this education to be provided in the home; this has caused much legal wrangling for years as to the minimum standards required for home education since the constitution does not explicitly provide for the State to define these minimum standards.

Pre-School Years

Pre-school is optional, in Ireland and takes the form of a number of privately run crèches, play-schools and Montessori schools, which children attend for one year or two, at ages three and/or four. Parents must pay to send their child to these institutions. From 2009 onwards children are entitled to a year of free pre-schooling in the year prior to starting primary schools under the "Early Childcare and Education Scheme".

Primary Years

The Primary School Curriculum consists of 8 years. They include Junior Infants (age 4-5/5-6), Senior Infants (age 5-6/6-7), First Class (age 6-7/7-8), Second Class (age 7-8/8-9), Third Class (age 8-9/9-10), Fourth Class (age 9-10/10-11), Fifth Class (age 10-11/11-12) and Sixth Class (age 11-12/12-13).

Secondary School

Secondary school has each of the following, the Junior cycle, Transition year and the Senior Cycle. The Junior cycle consits of First Year (age 12–14), Second Year (age 13-15) and Third Year (age 14-16)– The Junior Certificate examination is sat in all subjects (usually 10 or 11) in early June. Many schools hold Mock Examinations (also known as Pre-Certificate Examinations) to prepare students around February. The mocks are not state examinations — independent companies provide the exam papers and marking schemes – and are therefore not mandatory across all schools.

Transition Year is a one-year informal course which is taken by an increasing number of students usually ages 15 or 16. The content of this is left to the school to model on the local needs. It is compulsory in some schools, but optional in others and some schools do not have it. Students may attend structured classes, but do not cover material relevant to the Senior Cycle or the Leaving Certificate exams, and therefore students who choose not to do this year are in no way academically disadvantaged when entering the Senior Cycle. The range of activities in Transition Year or Fourth Year differs greatly from school to school, but many include activities such as work experience placements, project work, international trips or exchanges and excursions. Students may participate in courses such as creative writing, sailing, film-making, public speaking and so on, or enter competitions in science, fashion, motor sport and others which would normally be too time-consuming for a full-time student. Proponents of TY believe that it allows students an extra year to mature, engage in self-directed learning, explore career options and to choose subjects for senior cycle (the results of the Junior Certificate examination do not become available until midway through September, by which time students not taking Transition Year will already have chosen their classes and begun attending). Opponents believe that a year away from traditional study and the classroom environment can distract students and cause problems when they return to the Senior Cycle. They also believe that the activities undertaken in TY prevent some students from enrolling in this year, as they can be costly and most schools charge a fee of a few hundred euro to cover these activities.

And finally the Senior cycle which consists of Fifth Year (age 16–18 or if transition year is skipped age 15–17) and Sixth Year (age 17–19 or, again, if transition year is skipped age 16–18) – The Leaving Certificate examinations begin on the first Wednesday after the June bank holiday every year. Many schools have mocks to prepare students around February.

Further Education

Further education was for many years the "poor relation" of education[citation needed]. There were many different, often poorly defined[citation needed], awards offered by a multitude of bodies, both ad-hoc and statutory. Typical areas included apprenticeships, childcare, farming, retail, and tourism. These are typical areas of the economy that do not depend on multinational investment and recognition. There are many different types of further education awards, known as Post Leaving Certificates.

Law in Ireland

Law in Ireland

The constitution of ireland is the basic law of the state. It is known in Irelanda as Bunreacht Na hEireann.

As Ireland is a Catholic country, many of our laws are under the influence of the church's teaching.

The definition of rights covers five broad headings.

  • Personal Rights
  • The Family
  • Education
  • Private Property
  • Religion

Personal Rights

All citizens are equal, everyone has the right to express freely convictions peacefully without arms (weapons).

The Family

The state guarantees to protect your family- e.g. divorced or separated parents having equal acess to their child/children depending on their circumstances.

Education

Every child has the right to free primary education

Private Property

The right to hold your property is guaranteed.

Religion

The Irish state guarantees the freedom to practice your religious beliefs freely.

Citizens and in some cases non-citizens have the right to apply to be protected and have their rights guaranteed to them.

Court Systems

District court: Minor criminal offenses are dealt with here. It has the powers to impose a fine of up to 100 euro and prison sentences of up to two years.

Circuit Court: More serious offenses are dealt with here. They have the power to deal with all cases exept murder, piracy and allied offenses

High Court/ Central Criminal Court: The High Court can deal with all cases including both civil and criminal.

Special Criminal Court: This is all three courts sitting together.

Court of Criminal Appeal: Criminal appeals from the Circuit Court, the Central Court and the Special Criminal Court are heard here.

Supreme Court: The court of final appeal. The court hears appeals from the High Court of Criminal Appeal.

The Police

The police force in Ireland is called An Garda Siochana (meaning guardians of the piece). An Garda Siochana is a national force of approximately 10,500 members. The poliece force in Ireland are unarmed apart from some specialised units. The headquarters of the force is in Phoenix Park in Dublin.

An Garda Siochana training centre is located at Templemore in Co. Tipperary.

Defense

The role of the defense forces consist of the following:

  • To defend the state against armed agression.
  • To aid the civil power when called upon .
  • To participate in United Nations missions in the course of international peace

To provide a protection service in accordance with the state's obligations as a member of the European Union.

The forces may also be called upon to fulfill other desires e.g. search and rescue, air ambulance services, air transport, assistance on the occasion of a natural or other disasters, assistance in connection with the maintainence of essential services and combatting oil pollution at sea.

Mountains in Ireland

Carrantuohill:

Carrantuohill is the highest peak in Ireland, located in County Kerry. It is 1,038 metres high and is the central peak in Macgillycuddy's Reeks range. The range is composed of red sandstone, which has been modified be geological ice action.

Croagh Patrick

Croagh Patrick, nicknamed the reek, is a 764 metre high mountain and an important site of pilgrimage in County Mayo in Ireland. It is climbed by pilgrims on reek Sunday every year, which is the last Sunday in July. It's believed that it is good for your spirit to climb Croagh Patrick.

Mount Brandon

Mt. Brandon is a 952 metre high mountain on the Dingle Peninsula in Counrt Kerry, Ireland. Mt. Brandon is popular with Irish Catholic pilgrims. The path to the peak is marked by small white crosses and the peak itself is topped by a large metal cross. The route has the greatest concentration of stones marked with the "cross of arcs", one of the main symbols of pilgrimage in Ireland.

Benbulbin

Barm Brack

Barm brack (Irish: bairin breac), also called Barnbrack or often shortened to brack, is a yeasted bread with added sultanas and rasins. Usually sold in flattened rounds, it is often served toasted with butter along with a cup of tea in the afternoon. The dough is sweeter than sandwich bread, but not as rich as cake, and the sultanas and raisins add flavour and texture to the final product. In Ireland it is sometimes called Bairin Breac, and the term is used as two words i its more common version. This may come from the Irish word bairin-a loaf- and breac- speckled (due to the raisins in it), hence it means speckled loaf.

Colcannon

The Irish potato dish is a delicious combination of mashed potatoes, cooked cabbage, and optional green onions. People make these potatoes for Sunday dinner or an everyday family meal as a side dish to ham, roast beef, corned beef, or sausages. It's easy to prepare and wonderful any time of the year.

Scones

A small, light, biscuitlike quick bread made of oatmeal, wheatflour, barley meal, and sometimes have rasins and sultanas in it.

Irish stew

Irish stew is a traditional stew made from lamb, or mutton (mutton is from sheep over a year old and is less tender, is fattier, has a stronger flavour and is the most traditional meat to use) as well as potatoes, onions, and parsley. It may sometimes also include carrots.

L'Antiquité est la première des époques de l'Histoire. C'est par le développement ou l'adoption de l'écriture que l'Antiquité succède à la Préhistoire.


Vercingétorix deposant ses armes aux pieds de Jules César

Vercingétorix déposant les armes aux pieds de César après sa défaite à la bataille d’Alésia où il avait réuni tous les peuples Gaulois. Cela termine la conquête de la Gaule en 52 av. Moyen Âge

Le Moyen Âge s'étend de 476 à 1492. Il débuta avec l'effondrement de l'Empire romain d'Occident et se termina par la Renaissance et les Grandes découvertes. Située entre l'Antiquité et l'époque moderne, la période est subdivisée entre le haut Moyen Âge (VIe Xe siècle), le Moyen Âge central (XIe XIIIe siècle) et le Moyen Âge tardif (XIVe XVe siècle).

Portrait de Charlemagne

Charlemagne fut un empereur des VIIIe et IXe siècle

Charlemagne du latin Carolus Magnus, ou Charles Ier dit « le Grand », né en 742 et mort le 28 janvier 814 à Aix-la-Chapelle, est un membre de la dynastie franque à laquelle il a donné son nom alors qu'il n'en est pas le fondateur : les Carolingiens. Fils de Pépin le Bref, il est roi des Francs à partir de 768, devient par conquête roi des Lombards en 774 et est couronné empereur à Rome par le pape Léon III le 25 décembre 800. Monarque guerrier, il agrandit son royaume par une série de campagnes militaires, en particulier contre les Saxons païens dont la soumission a été très difficile et très violente (772-804), mais aussi contre les Lombards en Italie et les Musulmans d'Espagne.

Temps modernes

L'époque moderne (ou les temps modernes) couvre l'époque historique commençant à la fin du Moyen Âge, généralement placée en 1453 ou en 1492. Selon les historiens français, cette période s'arrête avec la Révolution française, marquée généralement en 1789 ou 1792, et c'est l'époque contemporaine qui lui succède.

Représentation de Louis XIV, le Roi Soleil

Louis XIV, roi de France au XVIIe siècle

Louis XIV dit Louis le Grand ou le Roi-Soleil, né le 5 septembre 1638 à Saint-Germain-en-Laye et mort le 1er septembre 1715 à Versailles, est un roi de France et de Navarre. Il règne sur le royaume de France du 14 mai 1643 à sa mort le 1er septembre 1715. Louis XIV monte en 1643 sur le trône au décès de son père Louis XIII, le 14 mai 1643, quelques mois avant ses cinq ans. Il est le 64e roi de France, le 44e roi de Navarre et le troisième des rois de la famille Bourbon. Son règne de 72 ans est l’un des plus longs de l'histoire de l'Europe. À partir de 1682, Louis XIV dirige son royaume depuis le vaste château de Versailles, modèle architectural de nombreux palais européens et dont il a dirigé la construction. Une cour soumet la noblesse, étroitement surveillée, à un protocole très élaborée. Le prestige culturel s'y affirme grâce au financement royal en faveur d'artistes tels que Molière, Racine, Boileau, Lully, Le Brun et Le Nôtre, ce qui favorise l'apogée du classicisme français, qualifié, dès son vivant, de « Grand Siècle », voire de « Siècle de Louis XIV ».

Époque contemporaine

L'époque contemporaine débute en 1789 et se poursuit jusqu'à nos jours. Quelques historiens font finir l'époque moderne en 1895 (invention du cinématographe) ou en 1896 (rénovation des Jeux olympiques), la période suivante et actuelle étant l'ère ou « l'époque de communication, spectacles et sports ». Mais ceci peut être contesté, la première moitié du XXe siècle étant l'aboutissement des idéologies et des nationalismes nés aux XVIIIe et XIXe siècles, les idéologies se radicalisant au XXe siècle et utilisant les médias, spectacles et sports. C'est aussi à l'époque contemporaine que se produit un accroissement démographique sans précédent dans l'histoire de l'humanité.

La prison royale de la Bastille est prise d'assaut

La prise de la Bastille lors de la Révolution Française le 14 juillet 1789. Cette date est devenue la fête nationale.

Cette révolution met fin à la monarchie absolue en France et elle est remplacée par une monarchie constitutionnelle en 1791. La Révolution française est à l’origine d’une déclaration des droits de l’homme et du citoyen qui transforme profondément la société de l’époque : les hommes sont désormais considérés comme libres et égaux en droits et la souveraineté émane désormais du peuple et non plus d’une monarchie de droit divin. La liberté de choisir sa religion est reconnue. La Révolution est aussi à l’origine des structures politiques modernes de la France : la France est découpée en départements, les poids et mesures sont unifiés sur tout le territoire français.

En 1792 la France devient une République, mais celle-ci est éphémère.

En 1799 le coup d’Etat du général Bonaparte signe la mort de la République.

La France connaît en réalité une très forte instabilité politique tout le long du XIXème siècle : entre 1791 et 1871 elle connaît : la monarchie constitutionnelle, le 1er empire et le second empire, 3 révolutions (les 3 Glorieuses en 1830, la révolution de 1848 et l’insurrection de la commune de Paris en 1871) et 2 républiques.

Napoléon Bonaparte, empereur des Français au XIXe

Napoléon Ier, né le 15 août 1769 à Ajaccio en Corse, dans le royaume de France, et mort le 5 mai 1821 sur l'île Sainte-Hélène, dans l'océan Atlantique, est le premier empereur des Français, du 18 mai 1804 au 6 avril 1814 et du 20 mars 1815 au 22 juin 1815. Second enfant de Charles Bonaparte et Letitia Ramolino, Napoléon Bonaparte est d'abord un militaire, général dans les armées de la Première République française, née de la Révolution, commandant en chef de l'armée d'Italie puis de l'armée d'Orient. Il parvient au pouvoir en 1799 par le coup d'État du 18 brumaire (9 novembre) et est Premier consul jusqu'au 2 août 1802, puis consul à vie jusqu'au 18 mai 1804, date à laquelle il est proclamé empereur. Enfin il est sacré empereur en la cathédrale Notre-Dame de Paris le 2 décembre 1804 par le pape Pie VII. En tant que général en chef et chef d'état, Napoléon tente de briser les coalitions montées et financées par le Royaume de Grande-Bretagne et qui rassemblent depuis 1792 les monarchies européennes contre la France et son régime né de la Révolution. Il conduit pour cela les armées françaises d'Italie au Nil et d'Autriche à la Prusse et à la Pologne : ses nombreuses et brillantes victoires (Arcole, Rivoli, Pyramides, Marengo, Austerlitz, Iéna, Friedland), dans des campagnes militaires rapides, disloquent les quatre premières coalitions. Les paix successives, qui mettent un terme à chacune de ces coalitions, renforcent la France et donnent à son chef, Napoléon, un degré de puissance jusqu'alors rarement égalé en Europe lors de la paix de Tilsit (1807).

Charles de Gaulle fut le 1er président de la Ve République, celle sous laquelle nous vivons actuellement.

Charles de Gaulle, né le 22 novembre 1890 à Lille et mort le 9 novembre 1970 à Colombey-les-Deux-Églises, est un général, résistant, écrivain et homme d'État français. Il est à la tête de la France du 3 juin 1944 au 20 janvier 1946 en tant que premier président du Gouvernement provisoire de la République française, puis du 8 janvier 1959 au 28 avril 1969 comme 18e président de la République française. Chef de la France libre puis dirigeant du Comité français de Libération nationale pendant la Seconde Guerre mondiale, président du Gouvernement provisoire de la République française de 1944 à 1946, président du Conseil des ministres français de 1958 à 1959, créateur de la Ve République fondée en 1958, il devient le 18e Président de la République française du 8 janvier 1959 au 28 avril 1969. Il est le premier à occuper la magistrature suprême sous la Cinquième République. Il rejette l'armistice demandé par Pétain à l'Allemagne nazie. De Londres, il lance, à la BBC, l'appel du 18 juin au peuple français pour résister et rejoindre les Forces françaises libres. Condamné à mort, et déclaré déchu de la nationalité Française par décret du 08 décembre 1940 par le régime de Vichy, il veut incarner la légitimité de la France et être reconnu en tant que puissance par les Alliés. Ne contrôlant que quelques colonies mais reconnu par la Résistance, il fusionne, en 1943, la France libre au sein du Comité français de Libération nationale, dont il finit par prendre la direction. Il dirige le pays à la Libération. Favorable à un exécutif fort, il s'oppose aux projets parlementaires des partis et démissionne en 1946. Il fonde le Rassemblement du peuple français (RPF), mais son refus de tout compromis avec le « régime des partis » l'isole dans une traversée du désert. De Gaulle revient au pouvoir lors de la crise du 13 mai 1958, pendant la guerre d'Algérie. Investi président du Conseil, il fait approuver la Ve République. Il décolonise aussi l'Afrique noire, en y maintenant l'influence française. De Gaulle prône l'« indépendance nationale » en rupture avec le fédéralisme européen et le partage de Yalta : il préconise donc une « Europe des nations » qui irait « de l'Atlantique à l'Oural », réalise la force de dissuasion nucléaire française, retire la France du commandement militaire de l'OTAN, pose un veto à l'entrée du Royaume-Uni dans la Communauté européenne, soutient le « Québec libre », condamne la guerre du Viêt Nam et reconnait la Chine communiste.
